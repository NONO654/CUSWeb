Overall Scoring

Data points are ranked automatically within Results Analytics based on requirements - Thresholds and Objectives - and Priority and Weight assigned to parameters and parameter groups. Since parameters can be grouped, scoring is hierarchical - the effects of priorities/weights are cascaded from groups to sub-groups to parameters, and all requirements are rolled up to determine an overall score for a data point. The relationship between priority and weight, and ability to adjust both, is described below, followed by the ranking method choices that use this information to produce the overall score, as well as scores at any level in the requirements hierarchy.

Calculating weight factors for groups and parameters

Two interdependent settings are used to control the final weight factor of any group or parameter: 1) priority (possible values are "MH" - Must Have, "P1", "P2", "P3", "P4", "P5", default value is "P5"), and 2) weight (possible values are in range [1...5], default value is 1.0). The value of priority is mainly used for setting the initial weight of a group or a parameter; the user can override the initial weight value by using the weight slider on the Collaborate page. The final weight factor of a parameter or a group is calculated as described below.

a.       The top level (root) parameter group always has a weight of 1.0

b.      For each sub-group or parameter within a group, the weight is determined from either the priority or the weight slider setting as follows:

i.      Initially, priority of the group or parameter is converted into its weight as follows:

  • priority "Must Have" and "P1" = weight of 5.0
  • priority "P2" = weight of 4.0
  • priority "P3" = weight of 3.0
  • priority "P4" = weight of 2.0
  • priority "P5" = weight of 1.0

ii.      The weight slider on the Elicit Preferences page overrides the initial priority setting to set the weight of the parameter or group (possible values in the range [1...5])

c.       All weights for sub-groups and parameters are added up to get a sum of weights within a given parent group

d.      Each weight for sub-groups and parameters is divided by the above sum to get a fraction of the total sum; this value is the fractional weight of a sub-group or a parameter

e.       The final weight factor of a group or a parameter is the product of its own fractional weight, and the fractional weights of all ancestor groups above this group or parameter; this final weight factor is used by the selected ranking method to rank the data points

 

Ranking Methods

The process of ranking alternatives in Results Analytics falls under the formal definition of Multi-Criteria Decision Making (MCDM) since in a majority of cases multiple conflicting objectives are present in a design problem and a compromise between various objectives is required. For example, the cost and weight of a produced part are normally in direct opposition to the strength, quality, and reliability of the final product. The difficulty of the problem is always increased by the presence of more than one criterion. There is no longer a single optimal solution to an MCDM problem that can be obtained without incorporating preference information. The concept of a single optimal solution is replaced by the set of non-dominated solutions where it is not possible to move away from such solution to any other without sacrificing in at least one criterion. Generally, however, the set of non-dominated solutions is too large to be presented to the decision maker for his final choice. Results Analytics helps the decision maker focus on his preferred solutions by applying preferences (priority and weight) and allowing the user to observe how the preferences affect the ranking of the alternatives (quick "tradeoff" studies). 

a.       Weighted Sum - the best known and simplest method for evaluating the goodness of multiple alternatives in terms of several objectives. The score value of each alternative is simply a sum of all objective values, each multiplied by the final weight factor calculated as described previously. For the Weighted Sum method to be valid all objectives must be normalized and brought into the same range, otherwise effects of different objective functions will not be equally represented.

b.       TOPSIS (Technique for Order of Preference by Similarity to Ideal Solution) - a multi-criteria ranking method that employs the concepts of the positive ideal point (best objective values for all criteria) and the negative ideal point (worst objective values in all criteria) in the objective domain. Alternatives are ranked based on the shortest geometric distance from the positive ideal point and the longest geometric distance from the negative ideal point in terms of the objective values. For the distance calculations of TOPSIS to be valid all objectives must be normalized to be in the same range of values. The final weight factors calculated as described previously are applied to each objective value when making distance calculations and then comparing alternatives.

TOPSIS will rank a balanced solution higher, even if the alternative is dominated, while Weighted Sum will rank a cutting edge or non-dominated solution higher.  Both ranking methods are provided to accommodate the decision maker's desired result.

 

Commonly asked questions: effects of scoring/ranking on data point display

The process of applying requirements to parameters to score and rank data points in addition to filtering capabilities all effect the data displayed in Results Analytics. These effects are described here in answers to commonly asked questions: 

  • What is the difference between Thresholds and parameter filters?

Data points that violate one or more of the thresholds are marked red. If the Infeasible filter is deselected, data points that violate one or more of the thresholds will not be displayed - they are filtered from view, but are still scored and ranked. When thresholds are set in the Parameter Definition table on the Define page, a marker T:(value) is displayed in the appropriate location on the parameter filter slider bar based on the threshold value and the data range below a lower threshold and/or above an upper threshold will be colored red in the filter slider bar.

Filter sliders for individual parameters allow filtering data points with values below a lower filter value or above an upper filter value from view. Filtered data points will not be displayed in the data table, scatter plots, etc., but are still scored and ranked, unlike excluded data points. Filtering data points based on parameter values will have no effect on defined thresholds. When a filter slider is moved, the slider bar data range below the lower filter or above the upper filter will be marked with diagonal hash marks indicating that this range has been filtered from view; scoring/rank of the filtered alternatives is unchanged.

  • What is the difference between Excluded Data Points and filtered data points?

Data points that have been explicitly excluded by the user are not graded: Rank and Score become zero and excluded data points are listed last, in gray italics, in the data table if displayed. If the Excluded filter is deselected, these user excluded data points will not be displayed - they are filtered from view. Excluded data points are also not included in surrogate model fitting (Predict page).

Filtered data points are only removed from view - they will not be displayed in the data table, scatter plots, etc. - but are still scored and ranked, unlike excluded data points. Filtered data points are included in surrogate model fitting (Predict page).

  • Why are there multiple Best Alternatives indicated?

Specification of multiple Objectives allows identification of all "Tradeoff" or "Pareto optimal" data points, which are colored green, to facilitate Multi-Criteria Decision Making (MCDM) as discussed above. If the Best Alternatives filter is deselected, these tradeoff/Pareto data points will not be displayed - they are filtered from view, but are still scored and ranked.

  • Why are Infeasible alternatives ranking above Best Alternatives ?:

Priority as defined above will affect scoring and ranking. By default the data points displayed in the data table are sorted by Rank, which is inversely related to Overall Score. If Infeasible and Best Alternatives are both displayed (not filtered from view) it is possible for red Infeasible data points to be ranked/scored higher, and as a result displayed higher in the data table, than green Best Alternatives . This can happen if the thresholds are not defined with MH 'Must Have' priority. Data points that violate a P1 - P5 priority threshold are not penalized for this violation and thus can have higher score based on objective values than green Best Alternatives with no violations. To penalize data points that do not meet thresholds , priority must be set to MH - 'Must Have'. MH priority will force the score to zero for data points that do not meet one or more threshold . By Using MH priorities, Best Alternatives will always be ranked higher than infeasible data points.
