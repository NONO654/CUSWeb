Calculating weight factors for groups and parameters

Two interdependent settings are used to control the final weight factor of any group or parameter: 1) priority (possible values are "MH" - Must Have, "P1", "P2", "P3", "P4", "P5", default value is "P5"), and 2) weight (possible values are in range [1...5], default value is 1.0). The value of priority is mainly used for setting the initial weight of a group or a parameter; the user can override the initial weight value by using the weight slider on the Elicit Preferences page. The final weight factor of a parameter or a group is calculated as described below.

a.       The top level (root) parameter group always has a weight of 1.0

b.      For each sub-group or parameter within a group, the weight is determined from either the priority or the weight slider setting as follows:

i.      Initially, priority of the group or parameter is converted into its weight as follows:

  • priority "Must Have" and "P1" = weight of 5.0
  • priority "P2" = weight of 4.0
  • priority "P3" = weight of 3.0
  • priority "P4" = weight of 2.0
  • priority "P5" = weight of 1.0

ii.      The weight slider on the Elicit Preferences page overrides the initial priority setting to set the weight of the parameter or group (possible values in the range [1...5])

c.       All weights for sub-groups and parameters are added up to get a sum of weights within a given parent group

d.      Each weight for sub-groups and parameters is divided by the above sum to get a fraction of the total sum; this value is the fractional weight of a sub-group or a parameter

e.       The final aggregated weight factor of a group or a parameter is the product of its own fractional weight, and the fractional weights of all ancestor groups above this group or parameter; this final weight factor is used by the selected ranking method to rank the data points

e.       The aggregated weight is used in scoring and shown in the Aggregated Weight chart in the Collaborate page and the Tree Map chart in the Recommend page.
